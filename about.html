Introduction

Dr. Xu Gong is currently working as Associate Research Scientist at the Center for Brain Disorder and Cognitive Science, College of Psychology, at Shenzhen University, Shenzhen Key Laboratory of Affective and Social Cognitive Science. She obtained her doctorate in Cognitive Neuroscience at the Center for Cognitive Neuroimaging, Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition, and Behaviour atRadboud University in the Netherlands and State Key Laboratory of Cognitive Neuroscience and Learning, Beijing Normal University.

Her research interest is in the field of Neuroeconomics or Decision Science (Neuroscience) in general, which combines interdisciplinary of Psychology, Economics, Cognitive Neuroscience, and Computer Science. Her research projects are focused on the understanding of psychological processes, motivations, and brain mechanisms of various kinds of human socioeconomic decision-making. She applies an interdisciplinary approach to integrating cognitive neuroimaging with computational modeling, psychological measures, behavioral economics, and clinical disciplines. She published articles on journals like Hormones and Behavior, Scientific Reports, PloS One, etc. She has been the reviewer of the Journal of Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, Neuropsychologia, PLoS One, Frontiers of Neuroscience, Frontiers of Psychology, Acta Psychologica Sinica, Artificial intelligence Review, etc.

Research

Research Questions: How we make choices (Decision Utilities)? What are the potential factors that contribute to our decisions? How can we integrate these factors into current decision models to help us better understand human decision-making? And How these decisive psychological/neural processes/experiences affect our subsequent behavior/emotions? In general, my research is trying to answer the above questions, which combines interdisciplinary of Psychology, Economics, Cognitive Neuroscience, and Computer Science. My research projects engaged in a deep understanding of the cognitive/emotional processes and brain mechanisms underlying human decision-making. Methodology: Our research integrates computational modeling and advanced statistical with multiple research approaches. For example, online experiments and questionnaires, behavioral laboratory experiments, neuroimaging experiments (EEG, fMRI, eye-tracking, etc.) as well as neuropharmacology and clinical research.

Social Decision-Making

Identifying the neural mechanisms of decision-making processes during social interactions (e.g., trust and reciprocity, social cooperation, social norm violation and altruistic punishment, fairness, and social equality).

Behavioral Economics/Economic Decision-making

Investigating the psychological mechanisms as it relates to the economic decision-making processes on an individual and societal level, providing empirical evidence for social policies. For example, do economic models match or maximize decision utility in real life? If not, how to help individuals maximize subjective expected utility? Investigating the underlying motivational mechanisms (eg., chronic stress, resource scarcity) of risk decision-making, investment decision-making, delay discounting, by the application of neuroeconomics paradigms and computational modeling approaches.

Emotion and Decision-making

Exploring the underlying mechanisms of how decisions influence emotions/emotional disorders and how they affect the subsequent decisions (eg., guilt, regret, gratitude, stress, anxiety, psychopathy), offering quantificational evidence for understanding the emotional/abnormal decisive behaviors or pathological mechanisms.

Cooperation

As we know, successful cooperation offers considerable benefits for ourselves and for others, but sometimes places us at risk of our cooperative acts being abused, and can be therefore a risky choice option. Although cooperation is important for the functioning of society, the origin of cooperation, especially in a competitive world, is still somewhat of a puzzle. Therefore, exploring and recognizing individual and situational factors that help in understanding cooperative decisions is of great importance.
